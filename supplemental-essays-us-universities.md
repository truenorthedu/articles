## Article

Once a student has submitted the Common App personal statement, the work is not finished. Most selective US universities require additional writing specific to their school. These are supplemental essays, and for students applying to eight, ten, or fifteen schools, the total word count across all supplements can easily exceed the personal statement by a factor of three or four.

Supplemental essays are where many applications get weaker, not stronger. Students put most of their energy into the personal statement, then rush through the supplements with generic answers that could apply to any school. Admissions readers notice this immediately.

This guide covers the main types of supplemental questions, how each one is meant to function, and what makes a response worth reading.

### Why supplemental essays exist

Each university designs its own supplemental questions to learn things the Common App cannot tell them. The personal statement is submitted to every school on a student's list. Supplemental essays let each school ask the specific questions that matter to their admissions process.

Some schools require a single short supplement. Others (Harvard, Yale, MIT, the University of Chicago, for example) require several responses across different categories, sometimes totaling 1,500 words or more. Students should check each school's requirements directly on their admissions page or through the Common App portal, as prompts and word limits change from cycle to cycle.

### The "Why Us" essay

The Why Us essay is the most common supplement type and the most frequently done badly.

The question is usually phrased as: "Why are you applying to [University]?" or "What draws you to [University]?" The word limit varies, typically between 100 and 300 words.

The purpose of this essay is specific: the school wants to know that the student has thought carefully about fit, not just prestige. A student who can describe exactly which professor they want to learn from, which research center connects to their interests, or which specific program structure appeals to them is signaling genuine engagement with the school. A student who writes about the campus being beautiful, the school having a great reputation, or the alumni network being strong is not.

The critical word in "Why Us" is "us." Not "why college in general" or "why a highly selective school." Why this school.

Weak Why Us essays tend to mention: the school's ranking, the school's name recognition, a general love of learning, and one or two facts that could have been found in a five-minute Google search. They sound interchangeable because they are.

Strong Why Us essays name specific things: a course in a specific department that connects to something the student already does or wants to explore; a professor whose published work or teaching they have actually looked into; a program, club, lab, or initiative that does not exist elsewhere in the same form; or a particular teaching style or curriculum structure (like UChicago's Core or Columbia's Literature Humanities sequence) that genuinely appeals to how the student thinks.

The research required to write a strong Why Us essay takes time. That is the point. Schools want applicants who want them specifically.

### The "Why Major" essay

Some schools ask separately: "Why do you want to study [major/field]?" This is related to the Why Us question but has a different purpose. Where Why Us is about fit with the institution, Why Major is about intellectual motivation.

The mistake here is narrating an interest rather than showing it. Writing "I have always been interested in economics because the world is shaped by financial systems" is narrating. Writing about a specific question in behavioral economics that the student read about, argued with, or tried to test in some way is showing.

Why Major essays work best when they contain:

- A specific moment, question, or experience that sharpened the student's interest
- Evidence that the interest is active, not just stated (a class, a project, a book, a competition, research)
- A clear connection to what the student wants to pursue at the university level

The essay does not need to be a career plan. Many strong applicants have no idea what career they want. What the reader is looking for is intellectual seriousness about the subject, not certainty about outcomes.

Some schools combine the Why Us and Why Major questions into one prompt. Students should read the prompt carefully before deciding how to allocate space between the two ideas.

### Community contribution essays

Many schools ask some version of: "What will you contribute to our community?" or "Describe a community you belong to and your role in it."

The word "community" here is broad on purpose. Students do not need to interpret it as school or geographic community. Community can mean a debate team, an online group of people working on the same problem, a family structure, a language community, or anything else where the student has a real role and real relationships.

These essays go wrong when students describe what they have received from a community rather than what they have contributed to it. The question is asking about direction: what does this student bring to the people around them?

Strong responses show a student who has taken some kind of initiative, supported others in a specific way, or contributed something that mattered to the group. The contribution does not need to be large. But it needs to be specific and real.

Generic answers about being "a good listener" or "always supportive" without specific examples tell the reader almost nothing.

### Short-answer activity descriptions

Some schools ask students to expand on one or more of their Common App extracurricular activities in short paragraphs of 100 to 250 words. These are not separate essays in the traditional sense, but they require the same discipline: specificity over summary.

The Common App activity list gives 150 characters per entry, which forces compression. The short-answer expansion is a chance to go deeper on what the activity actually involved and what the student actually did, thought about, or contributed.

A student who spent three years working on a research project should not summarize the project in a way that sounds like a resume bullet. They should pick one specific aspect, problem, or discovery that shows what doing the work was actually like.

The same applies to any other activity. Describing the category of thing is less useful than describing one specific moment, decision, or outcome within it.

### Diversity essays

Diversity essays ask students to reflect on a part of their identity, background, or experience that has shaped their perspective. Prompts vary. Some are direct ("Describe how your background has influenced your perspective"). Others are more open ("Tell us about something that is important to you and why").

Students sometimes avoid this essay type, worried that they do not have a story that "qualifies." That is usually the wrong interpretation of the question. Schools are not looking for students from specific demographics. They are looking for students who can reflect meaningfully on where they come from and how that has shaped the way they think.

Strong diversity essays tend to be specific about context rather than vague about identity. The goal is not to state a category but to show what belonging to that category, or growing up in that context, actually felt like and what the student carried from it.

### One essay per school, not one template applied everywhere

The most common error across all supplement types is using a template and swapping in the school name. Admissions readers review thousands of applications for their school. They can tell when a Why Us essay was written for another school and edited. They can tell when the details are thin, the research is superficial, and the interest is performed.

Each school deserves its own answer, built from actual research into the school, not from general enthusiasm about the type of school it represents.

This is time-consuming. Students applying to fifteen schools need to write fifteen Why Us essays, each one genuinely specific to that institution. There is no shortcut that works.

### Planning the supplements across a school list

The total supplemental writing load is worth calculating before finalizing a college list. A student applying to twelve schools, each requiring two to three supplements plus a personal statement, may be writing 8,000 to 12,000 words total across the application cycle. That takes time, and rushing at the end is where quality drops.

Students benefit from starting supplemental research and drafting early in the fall, not after the personal statement is finished. Some students draft Why Us responses over the summer, once the school list is more settled.

## FAQ

**Q: How many supplemental essays will I need to write?**
It depends on your school list. Some schools require no supplements beyond the personal statement. Others require four or five separate responses. Many of the most selective schools fall in the higher range. Check each school's requirements in the Common App portal or on the school's admissions website directly, as requirements change each cycle.

**Q: Can I reuse sections of one supplemental essay in another?**
Sometimes, with care. Background on your interest in a subject might transfer across multiple Why Major essays. But Why Us essays should almost never share content between schools. If you find yourself copying and pasting a Why Us response, the essay is probably not specific enough to work for either school.

**Q: What is the right length for a supplemental essay?**
Use the word limit as a target, not a cap. If the limit is 250 words, aim for 220 to 250. Going significantly under the limit usually signals that you have not said enough. Going over the limit is not allowed.

**Q: How specific do I need to be in a Why Us essay?**
As specific as possible. Naming a professor, a course number, a named research lab, a specific student organization, or a curriculum feature gives the reader evidence that you have actually looked into the school. General statements about "the community" or "the opportunities" do not.

**Q: Do I need to write a different Why Major essay for each school?**
If your major is the same across all schools, the core content can overlap. But each school's program has different features, faculty, and structure. A strong Why Major essay connects your intellectual interests to what that school's department actually offers. Some customization is almost always necessary.

**Q: When should I start writing supplemental essays?**
Start researching schools and drafting Why Us notes as early as the summer before Grade 12. Finalize supplements in the fall, well before deadlines. November 1 is the common Early Decision and Early Action deadline for many selective schools, which leaves very little time if you start in October.
