## The cost question families ask first

For families in Asia considering US universities, the first question is often not "where should we apply?" It's "how much will this actually cost, and can we make it work?"

US universities are expensive. Annual costs at many private colleges, including tuition, accommodation, and living expenses, can exceed USD 80,000. For families who did not grow up in the US system, the scholarship and financial aid landscape can seem opaque or intimidating.

But the system is more navigable than it looks. Several US universities offer serious financial support to international students, and some offer more than families expect. The challenge is knowing where to look, what the different types of support are, and how to build a school list that reflects financial reality alongside academic ambition.

---

## Quick answer

International students are not eligible for US federal financial aid. The main sources of financial support are institutional need-based grants, which many private universities award based on a family's demonstrated financial need, and merit scholarships, which universities award based on academic achievement or talent regardless of financial need. The CSS Profile is the main application tool for institutional need-based aid at around 400 colleges. A small number of elite universities are need-blind for international applicants, meaning financial need does not affect admission decisions. Most universities are need-aware, meaning financial circumstances can play a role.

## US federal aid: not available to most international students

Before looking at what is available, it helps to be clear on what is not.

The Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA) is the gateway to US federal grants and loans. For most international students, FAFSA is not an option. Students holding F-1 or J-1 student visas are not eligible for federal student aid programs, according to the US Department of Education's studentaid.gov. Only certain non-citizen categories, including lawful permanent residents (green card holders) and specific refugee or parole visa holders, can qualify.

This does not mean financial support is unavailable. It means the relevant support comes from universities themselves, not the federal government.

## The two main types of support: need-based aid and merit scholarships

### Need-based financial aid

Need-based aid is awarded by universities to families who demonstrate financial need. The amount depends on what the institution calculates the family can afford to contribute, compared with the total cost of attendance.

At universities that practice need-based aid for international students, the main application tool is the **CSS Profile**, administered by College Board. Around 400 colleges and scholarship programs use the CSS Profile to assess a family's financial situation for institutional (non-federal) aid. International students can complete it using their home currency, and College Board applies a global need analysis that accounts for the family's economic context relative to the US economy.

The CSS Profile asks detailed questions about parental income, assets, home ownership, savings, and family circumstances. It goes further than the FAFSA and is more complex. Families should start gathering financial documents early, as the forms take time to complete carefully.

One important distinction: not all universities that offer need-based aid to international students will fully cover the demonstrated need. Some meet 100% of demonstrated need, meaning the gap between the family contribution and total cost is fully covered by grants and work-study. Others offer partial need-based grants. This difference matters significantly when comparing offers.

### Merit scholarships

Merit scholarships are awarded on the basis of academic performance, test scores, or other demonstrated achievements, and they are not tied to financial need. A family earning a high income can still receive a merit scholarship.

Many private universities automatically consider international applicants for merit scholarships during the admissions process. Amounts vary widely. Some awards cover a fixed sum each year, such as USD 10,000 to USD 27,000. Others cover full tuition or even full cost of attendance, though these are competitive and limited.

Some state universities also offer merit aid to international students, though the amounts tend to be smaller than at well-resourced private institutions. It is worth checking the specific merit aid policies of any public university on a student's list.

## Need-blind versus need-aware: a critical distinction

This is one of the most important concepts in understanding how financial aid intersects with admissions for international students.

**Need-blind admissions** means a university does not consider a student's financial circumstances when deciding whether to admit them. The admissions decision and the financial aid process are entirely separate.

**Need-aware admissions** means the university does take financial need into account, at least for some portion of the applicant pool. At a need-aware institution, an international student who applies for substantial financial aid may face a slightly more difficult path to admission than a full-pay student with a similar academic profile.

As of 2025-2026, a small number of US universities are need-blind for international students. The most commonly cited are Harvard, Princeton, Yale, MIT, Amherst, Bowdoin, Dartmouth, and Brown (which became need-blind for international students beginning with its Class of 2029). Policies can and do change. Always verify directly with the university's financial aid office before treating any list as definitive.

The vast majority of US universities, including many highly regarded ones, are need-aware for international students. This does not mean they offer no aid. It means the financial profile of an applicant can be a factor in the decision.

## Which types of institutions tend to offer the most support?

In general, well-endowed private liberal arts colleges and research universities tend to have the largest institutional aid budgets. Schools with large endowments have more resources to distribute, and some have made genuine commitments to meeting international students' demonstrated need.

Large public universities, by contrast, typically direct most of their financial support toward domestic students. Some offer competitive merit awards for high-achieving international applicants, but need-based institutional aid for international students is less common.

A few points that can help families assess a school's financial aid generosity:

- **Does the school meet 100% of demonstrated need for international students?** This is different from simply offering aid. A school that meets full need leaves no gap between the family contribution and total costs.
- **Does the school offer need-based aid at all to international students?** Some schools only offer merit scholarships to international applicants. Others offer both.
- **What is the average aid package for international students?** Some universities publish this data, which gives a useful indicator of typical award sizes.

## How families can plan their list with financial fit in mind

Building a list without thinking about cost is common, but it creates problems later. A student who is admitted to a school they cannot afford ends up either taking on substantial debt or declining the offer. Neither outcome serves the family well.

A more practical approach is to treat financial aid as a filter when building the school list, rather than as an afterthought after decisions arrive.

This means including at least a few schools known for generous aid to international students alongside academic targets. It also means reading each school's international financial aid page carefully before adding it to the list. Some schools state clearly that they offer limited or no institutional aid to international students, which is important information before investing time in the application.

Families should be realistic about what aid they are likely to receive. A school that offers merit scholarships does not guarantee every strong applicant will receive one. A school that practices need-based aid will calculate the family contribution using its own methodology, which may differ from what the family expects.

Being upfront about financial need early in the process, rather than hoping to negotiate later, tends to produce better outcomes.

## A note for parents

Many parents from Asia approach the US college process as primarily an academic exercise, focusing on rankings and name recognition. Financial planning often comes later. But the admissions and financial aid systems are connected. Applying for aid at a need-aware school is a different strategic decision than applying to a need-blind school.

Parents benefit from understanding:

- Which schools on the list are need-blind versus need-aware for international students
- Whether the family will apply for need-based aid, and at which schools
- What merit scholarship opportunities exist and what the typical award amounts are
- What a realistic family contribution looks like, separate from the sticker price

This is not a reason to limit ambition. It is a reason to plan thoughtfully.

## Common mistakes families make

**Assuming all universities offer the same level of aid.** They do not. Policies vary significantly, and a school at a similar ranking level to another may offer dramatically different support to international students.

**Waiting until after admission to research financial aid.** By the time a decision arrives, the financial aid process is often already in motion or closed. Deadlines for CSS Profile submission can be as early as November for Early Decision applicants.

**Conflating rankings with aid generosity.** Some highly ranked schools offer limited need-based aid to international students. Some lower-ranked schools offer more. Aid policy is a separate consideration from academic prestige.

**Applying for aid late.** Many schools have separate financial aid deadlines. Submitting the CSS Profile after a school's aid deadline can mean a student is considered for admission but not for institutional support.

## Where to start

The clearest first step is to research the financial aid policies of every school on a student's preliminary list. The College Board's CSS Profile website (cssprofile.collegeboard.org) provides an updated list of member institutions. Each school's financial aid page for international students is the most accurate source of information on what they offer.

For families beginning this process, starting with a clear picture of what the family can realistically contribute before building the list, rather than after, tends to produce the most useful outcomes.

## FAQ

**Can international students apply for FAFSA?**
Most international students cannot. FAFSA eligibility generally requires US citizenship or lawful permanent residency. Students on F-1 or J-1 visas are not eligible for US federal student aid. International students should focus on institutional aid offered directly by universities, which is what the CSS Profile is used for.

**What is the CSS Profile and who needs to complete it?**
The CSS Profile is a financial aid application administered by College Board. Around 400 colleges and scholarship programs use it to assess families' financial situations for institutional (non-federal) need-based aid. Many colleges require international applicants to complete the CSS Profile alongside their admissions application if they are applying for financial aid.

**Are need-blind schools automatically more generous?**
Not necessarily. Need-blind means financial need does not affect the admissions decision. Whether the school meets full demonstrated need is a separate question. Some need-blind schools meet 100% of need; others may admit a student regardless of need but offer a smaller aid package. Always check both policies.

**Do merit scholarships require a separate application?**
It depends on the school. Some universities automatically consider all admitted international applicants for merit awards without a separate application. Others require students to apply for specific scholarship programs. Check each school's website for details.

**How does need-aware admissions work in practice?**
At a need-aware institution, the admissions committee may consider a student's financial aid application as part of the overall assessment, particularly in borderline cases or when the institution is near its aid budget limit. The practical effect varies by school and by applicant pool. Strong academic candidates are generally less affected than borderline cases.

**When should families start the financial aid process?**
Families should begin researching each school's international aid policy at the same time they start building the school list, ideally in Grade 11. The CSS Profile typically becomes available in October of the student's final year (Grade 12), and many schools have aid deadlines in November or December.
