Every summer, families face a version of the same question: what should my child be doing this summer to help their university application?

The honest answer is more nuanced than most programme brochures suggest. Summer can be one of the best opportunities a student has to develop real academic depth and produce something meaningful. But the way students and families typically approach summer planning often misses what admissions committees are actually looking for.

## The quick answer

Selective, output-driven experiences matter. Brand-name pre-college programmes at Harvard, Yale, and similar universities do not signal admission potential and are not treated as credentials by those universities' own admissions offices. What admissions committees look at is whether a student pursued something genuinely challenging, whether there was a competitive selection process, and whether the student produced or developed something real. The question is not which programme looks most impressive on paper. It is what the student actually did.

## Why the Harvard summer programme does not help you get into Harvard

This is worth stating directly, because many families invest considerable money based on a misunderstanding.

Pre-college summer programmes at Harvard, Yale, Brown, Columbia, and most other selective US universities are, in the majority of cases, open-enrolment programmes. Students apply and are accepted at very high rates. Some are primarily designed as revenue-generating programmes, not selective academic experiences.

Harvard's own admissions office has stated publicly that attending its pre-college programmes carries no weight in the undergraduate admissions process. The same applies to similar programmes at peer institutions. Attending one of these programmes does not create a connection to the university. It does not mean the university has evaluated your academic potential. Admissions officers at these schools are well aware of how their own programmes work.

A student who attends a paid, open-enrolment pre-college programme and lists it on their application as a credential is likely to be seen as having misunderstood the admissions process, which is itself a signal.

## What admissions committees actually look for in summer activities

The criteria that matter are consistent, and they apply whether a student spent their summer in a formal programme, at a research institution, or working on something independently.

**Selectivity.** Was there a genuine application process with meaningful rejection? A programme that accepts 30% or fewer applicants, based on academic performance or demonstrated interest, carries more weight than one that accepts anyone who pays.

**Intellectual challenge.** Did the student engage with material or problems that stretched them? A six-week research programme where a student struggles to understand a field and grows through that struggle is more valuable than a comfortable seminar.

**Tangible output.** Did the student produce something? A research paper, a dataset, a software project, a published piece of writing, a report, a portfolio of work. Programmes that end with a certificate of completion and no product are harder to speak to in essays or interviews. Programmes that require students to produce and present something give the student real material to discuss.

**Genuine connection to the student's academic interests.** The most important test is whether this experience fits the student's developing intellectual identity. A student interested in economics who spent a summer doing economics research is showing follow-through. A student who attended three different unrelated programmes in three summers is showing calendar management.

## Selective and output-focused programmes worth knowing

These programmes are competitive, rigorous, and produce real academic experiences.

**Research Science Institute (RSI)** at MIT is one of the most competitive pre-college research programmes in the world. Students conduct original research with faculty mentors over six weeks and present their work. Acceptance is genuinely selective.

**MIT PRIMES** is a year-long research programme for high school students with strong mathematics and computer science backgrounds. The summer component runs alongside an academic year commitment.

**Johns Hopkins Center for Talented Youth (CTY)** runs competitive-track programmes that require strong test score qualifications. Not all CTY programmes carry the same weight, so students should look at the specific track and its requirements.

**Stanford Institutes of Medicine Summer Research Program (SIMR)** allows students to conduct laboratory research at Stanford. The application process is competitive and the experience is substantive.

**Intel ISEF-affiliated state and regional fairs** are not summer programmes in the traditional sense, but doing original research over the summer to develop a project that qualifies for science fair competition is one of the strongest things a high school student can do.

There are strong programmes in other countries and regions as well. Students in Asia should look at research opportunities at local universities, national science academies, and organisations that run supervised independent research. These carry real weight when the research is genuine and the student can speak to it clearly.

## The value of working with a professor or researcher directly

One option that families often overlook is reaching out to university faculty directly. A student with genuine interest in a field can write to professors, explain their interest, and ask whether there are opportunities to assist with research, even in a limited capacity.

This is more work than enrolling in a programme. It requires initiative, persistence, and a clear sense of what the student wants to explore. But it is also exactly the kind of initiative that admissions essays and interviews can speak to honestly. A student who arranged their own research placement by reaching out to five professors, got one response, and spent the summer learning a new methodology has a story that is much more interesting than "I attended a pre-college programme."

The same principle applies to independent projects, community initiatives the student organised themselves, or meaningful internships that involved real responsibilities. These experiences are only valuable if the student can explain concretely what they did, what they learned, and what they produced or contributed.

## Short programmes versus longer, deeper engagements

A one-week or two-week programme rarely gives a student enough time to do anything substantive. Students attend, sit in lectures, meet peers, and go home with a certificate. There is nothing wrong with this socially, but it does not produce the kind of experience a student can speak to meaningfully in a college essay or interview.

Longer engagements, typically six weeks or more, allow for actual development. The student has time to struggle, to adjust, to improve, and to finish something. That arc is what produces material for authentic writing and genuine academic growth.

If a family is weighing a two-week programme against a six-week programme in the same area, the longer one is almost always a better investment of the student's time.

## For international students in Asia

Students in Asia have strong options that are often underrated by families fixated on US-branded programmes.

Research programmes at universities in Singapore, Hong Kong, Japan, South Korea, and other countries offer supervised research experiences with faculty. National science academies and government-supported youth research programmes exist in many countries and are genuinely selective.

Subject olympiad preparation programmes, when they feed into a national team qualification process, represent an extremely credible form of summer intellectual engagement. A student who spent their summer preparing for and qualifying for a national olympiad team has demonstrated far more than any pre-college programme could.

Regional academic competitions with summer research components, supervised independent study with a local mentor, or a substantial community project the student designed and led are all legitimate options that admissions officers at US and UK universities understand and respect.

The question to ask is always the same: would I be able to explain in concrete detail what I did, why I did it, and what I learned or produced?

## Common mistakes families make when planning summers

Treating summer as a checkbox exercise. Selecting activities because they look impressive rather than because they fit the student. Attending multiple short, unrelated programmes and assuming the quantity signals effort. Paying for a pre-college programme at a target university in the belief that it strengthens the application to that university.

Another common mistake is starting too late. Students in Grade 9 and 10 have more flexibility to explore broadly. Students in Grade 11 should be using their summers to deepen work in an area they are already developing. A student who switches directions in the summer before their application year and starts something entirely new will find it harder to tell a coherent story.

The students whose summer experiences read most compellingly in applications are the ones who were pursuing something because they genuinely wanted to, not because someone told them it would look good.

## A note for parents

It is natural to want to identify the right programme and enrol. Parents often carry more anxiety about this than students do, and the industry of paid pre-college programmes is well designed to respond to that anxiety with clear products, prestigious names, and easy enrolment.

The more useful question to sit with is: what is my child actually curious about, and how do we find or create an experience that gives them real engagement with that? The answer may be a selective programme, a faculty research placement, an independent project, or a competition. It is rarely a paid brand-name seminar at a university your child wants to attend.

## What to do next

Start by identifying what your child is genuinely curious about. Then look for programmes or opportunities, selective ones, longer ones, output-focused ones, that give them real engagement in that area. If the programme requires a competitive application, that is a good sign. If it does not, ask why.

Students in Grades 9 and 10 have room to explore. Students in Grade 11 should be deepening. Both should be able to describe what they did and what they produced.

## FAQ

**Do summer programmes at Harvard or Yale help you get admitted to those schools?**

No. Pre-college summer programmes at Harvard, Yale, and most other selective US universities are, in the majority of cases, open-enrolment programmes. Harvard's admissions office has confirmed that these programmes carry no weight in the undergraduate admissions process. Admissions officers are fully aware that these are not selective experiences.

**What makes a summer programme worth including on a university application?**

Selectivity, intellectual challenge, length, and tangible output. A competitive application process, substantive academic engagement, and a product or result the student can speak to are the markers that matter. A programme that accepts most applicants and ends with a certificate of participation is unlikely to add value.

**Is it better to do one strong summer programme or several shorter ones?**

One longer, deeper engagement is almost always more valuable than several short programmes. Six weeks or more gives a student time to produce something real and to experience genuine intellectual challenge. Two-week programmes rarely allow for that.

**What are some well-regarded selective summer programmes?**

Research Science Institute (RSI), MIT PRIMES, Johns Hopkins CTY competitive tracks, and Stanford SIMR are among the programmes with competitive admissions and substantive academic content. Students doing serious research that qualifies for ISEF competitions are also in strong territory.

**My child is in Asia. Are local summer programmes respected by US and UK admissions officers?**

Yes, when they are selective and substantive. Research placements at local universities, national olympiad preparation programmes, and supervised independent study can all be strong options. Admissions officers evaluate the experience itself, not the country it happened in.

**When should students start thinking about summer planning?**

Ideally, students in Grade 9 begin developing a sense of their academic interests so that by Grade 10 and 11 they can pursue deeper experiences. Leaving summer planning to the spring of the relevant year often means missing application deadlines for competitive programmes, many of which close in January or February.
