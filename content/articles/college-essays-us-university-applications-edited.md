## Article

Most students spend weeks choosing the perfect topic for their college essay. Then they write an essay that could belong to anyone.

The college essay is 650 words. It sits at the center of the Common App. And while it will not get a student into a highly selective school on its own, a weak essay can quietly hurt an otherwise strong application. More importantly, a strong essay does something grades and test scores cannot: it shows the reader who the student actually is.

This article covers the Common App personal statement only. Supplemental essays (the school-specific questions) are covered separately.

### What the Common App personal essay is

The Common App personal statement is a single essay of up to 650 words submitted to every school on a student's list. Students choose from seven prompts, though the prompts are deliberately open-ended. Most strong essays could technically fit under several of them.

The essay goes to every admissions reader across every college on the list, so it needs to represent the student broadly, not respond to the specifics of any one school.

Common App updates its prompts occasionally. Students should check the official Common App website at commonapp.org for the current cycle's options before settling on a direction.

### Why it matters in holistic admissions

US college admissions at selective schools is holistic, meaning no single factor determines the outcome. Readers look at academic performance, course rigor, test scores (where required), extracurricular involvement, recommendations, and essays together.

The essay is the one part of the application a student writes entirely themselves, with a direct voice. It is the best chance to give the admissions reader a sense of the person behind the transcript.

Admissions offices at selective schools are reading thousands of applications. Many applicants have strong grades, strong scores, and a respectable list of activities. The essay is often where one student becomes more memorable than another.

### Choosing a topic

The most common mistake is picking a topic that sounds impressive rather than one that is revealing.

Essays about winning championships, overcoming a dramatic obstacle, or traveling to help communities abroad are not automatically strong. They can be strong. But only if the writing reveals genuine self-awareness, specific detail, and a clear sense of who the student is. Most of the time, these topics become generic precisely because the student focuses on the event itself rather than what they actually thought, felt, or changed.

The better question is not "What is my most impressive experience?" It is closer to "What would help a reader understand something real about how I think, what I care about, or what shaped the way I see things?"

Strong essays have been written about:

- Learning to bake bread with a grandparent
- A character from a book that the student argued with for years
- The specific way a student approaches a hobby most people consider trivial
- A mistake and what it actually took to sit with it
- A small, specific moment that only makes sense once you understand how the student thinks

The topic does not need to be dramatic. It needs to be specific and honest.

### Finding an authentic voice

Admissions readers can tell when an essay has been written to impress rather than to communicate. The language becomes elevated in a way that feels performed. The tone shifts away from how a 17-year-old actually thinks. The conclusions feel too neat.

Voice is not just word choice. It is the specific way a person notices things, makes connections, and expresses ideas. The goal is to write in a way that sounds like the student, not like what the student thinks a college essay should sound like.

One practical test: read the essay aloud. If it sounds like a speech, or like a news article, or like a document, it probably needs to loosen up. If it sounds like a version of how the student actually talks when they are explaining something they care about, that is closer.

This does not mean the essay should be casual or unpolished. It means the writing should reflect a real person, not a persona.

### Structure and flow

There is no single correct structure for a college essay. But certain structural choices tend to work better than others.

**Opening with a specific scene or detail** is often more effective than opening with a general statement. Starting with "I have always been passionate about science" tells the reader almost nothing. Starting with a specific moment pulls the reader in and earns the abstract claim later.

**The middle of the essay** should deepen the picture. This is where the student shows what they actually think, not just what happened to them. Reflection matters here, but it should feel earned from the specific details rather than attached to the end as a conclusion.

**The ending** does not need to wrap everything up. Many strong essays end with a smaller, quieter moment rather than a summary of what the student learned. The reader does not need to be told the lesson in the final sentence. If the essay has done its job, the reader already understands it.

Avoid overly long introductions. Admissions readers move quickly. Get to something real within the first few sentences.

### What does not work

A few patterns show up consistently in weak college essays:

**Writing about the activity instead of the person.** An essay about debate club that spends most of its words describing the competition format and the student's record tells the reader about debate, not about the student. The activity is context, not the point.

**Trying to cover too much.** 650 words is not enough space for a timeline of growth, three formative experiences, and a reflection on future goals. Essays that try to do this end up being thin about all of it. Narrower is almost always better.

**Excessive humility.** Some students open by explaining that they are not exceptional, that their topic is small, or that they know this essay does not make them stand out. These disclaimers are not necessary and undercut the essay before it begins.

**Over-edited prose.** When too many people give feedback over too many rounds, essays can lose the student's voice entirely. The writing becomes grammatically clean but oddly flat. One or two trusted readers is usually enough.

**The insight that appears only at the end.** If the reflection in the final paragraph feels disconnected from the specific details in the body, the essay has not done its work. The insight should grow from the details, not be added afterward.

### What parents should know

Parents naturally want to help, and editing suggestions are often useful. But the essay should sound like the student, not like a parent's idea of what a college essay should sound like. The most common parental edits that hurt essays are: making the language more formal, adding qualifications that soften the student's natural voice, and pushing toward topics that seem more "impressive."

The student is the one applying. The reader is trying to get to know the student. If the essay sounds like a parent wrote it, that goal is lost.

## FAQ

**Q: How long should the college essay be?**
The Common App allows up to 650 words. Most strong essays are between 550 and 650 words. Going significantly shorter is fine if the essay is complete, but most students find they need the full space to develop their idea properly. Do not pad the essay to hit the limit.

**Q: Can I reuse the same Common App personal statement for every school?**
Yes. The Common App personal statement goes to all schools on your list automatically. You do not submit different versions to different schools. School-specific writing happens in the supplemental essays.

**Q: How do I know if my topic is good enough?**
There is no list of approved topics. A topic is strong if it gives the reader a clear, specific, honest picture of who you are. The topic matters less than what you do with it. Many seemingly ordinary topics produce the strongest essays.

**Q: Should I write about a challenge or hardship?**
Only if it is genuinely the most revealing thing you can write about. Admissions readers see many essays about overcoming difficulty. What distinguishes strong ones is not the difficulty itself but the specificity of the reflection. If something difficult genuinely shaped you in a way no other topic could capture, write about it. If you are choosing it because it seems like it will impress readers, a different topic will probably work better.

**Q: How many drafts should I write?**
Most students need at least two or three drafts before the essay feels close. A first draft is about getting the idea down. Revision is where the voice and structure develop. Do not try to write a final essay in one sitting.

**Q: When should I start writing the college essay?**
Many students benefit from starting the personal essay in the summer before Grade 12. That gives enough time to draft, step away, revise, and have a few readers look at it without the pressure of a fast-approaching deadline.
