## Introduction

Grade 10 is not the moment to panic. It is also not the moment to ignore university planning entirely. It sits in a useful middle ground: early enough that good decisions now will compound over the next two years, but not so early that everything needs to be locked in immediately.

Many families arrive at Grade 11 or Grade 12 feeling behind. Some of that feeling is unavoidable, because Grade 11 is genuinely demanding. But some of it comes from not using Grade 10 well. This article explains what Grade 10 preparation should actually look like, without overstating the urgency or underselling what this year can do.

---

## Why Grade 10 matters more than families realise

When students apply to US universities in the fall of Grade 12, or to UK universities through UCAS in the autumn of Year 13, they are submitting two years of high school evidence: their Grade 11 and Grade 12 records. Grade 10 itself does not typically appear prominently in university transcripts for US applications, but the choices made in Grade 10 shape what Grade 11 looks like. Course selection, extracurricular direction, and academic habits built in Grade 10 are what produce a strong Grade 11 record.

For UK university applications, Grade 10 is even more structurally significant. Students in the UK equivalent (Year 11) are sitting their GCSEs during this period. GCSE results feed directly into predicted grades, which are the grades UK universities make offers on. A student who underperforms in Year 11 GCSEs will face harder questions when teachers write their Year 13 predicted grades.

In short: Grade 10 is the year that sets the conditions for everything that follows.

## Academic course selection: choosing deliberately, not by default

One of the most important decisions in Grade 10 is which courses to take in Grade 11. This applies whether the student is in an IB school, an A-Level school, an AP curriculum, or a hybrid. The choice matters for two reasons.

First, universities will see these courses on transcripts and predicted grades. Taking the most challenging courses available in a student's genuine areas of strength signals something different from taking easy options. Second, the courses chosen in Grade 11 often lock in an academic direction. An IB student selecting their six Higher and Standard Level subjects, or an A-Level student choosing their three or four subjects, is making a decision that shapes what university courses they can plausibly apply to.

Grade 10 is the right time to think through this with some care. Questions worth asking:

- Does the student have a sense of what subjects they find genuinely interesting, not just what they are good at?
- Are there subject areas they might want to pursue at university that require a specific prerequisite course?
- Is the student choosing courses based on reputation for being "easy" or based on actual interest and university relevance?

No final answers are needed in Grade 10, but the conversation should happen. A student who arrives at Grade 11 course selection without having thought about it is at a disadvantage compared to one who has spent some time considering options.

## The PSAT in Grade 10: a useful diagnostic

Many students do not realise that the PSAT 10 (or PSAT/NMSQT for some) is available in Grade 10. This is not a high-stakes test. It does not affect university admissions. But it is a useful data point.

The PSAT in Grade 10 gives families and students a realistic sense of where a student stands relative to the SAT — the test that does matter for US university applications. A student who sits the PSAT in Grade 10 and reviews the results can identify specific areas in math or reading that need attention, and build that work into Grade 10 and Grade 11 before the SAT matters.

There is no pressure to start intensive SAT or ACT preparation in Grade 10. Many students begin that preparation more seriously in Grade 11. But using the PSAT as a diagnostic, understanding roughly where you sit, and building awareness of the testing timeline is sensible preparation.

## Extracurricular focus: starting to narrow

In Grade 8 and Grade 9, a degree of exploration is healthy. Students try different activities, join different clubs, and develop a broader sense of what interests them. Grade 10 is often the right moment to start narrowing that exploration into something more focused.

This does not mean dropping everything but one activity. It means beginning to think about which one or two areas feel genuinely meaningful, rather than collecting activities for a list. US university applications, particularly to selective schools, are not impressed by long lists of superficial involvement. They respond to evidence of genuine engagement, growing responsibility, and impact in specific areas.

A student who has been playing in the school orchestra since Grade 7 and takes on a leadership role or starts teaching younger students in Grade 10 is building a different kind of story from one who joins ten clubs in Grade 11 to fill a resume. The same principle applies to community service, research, entrepreneurship, sports, or any other area.

Grade 10 is the time to ask: of everything this student is involved in, what actually matters to them? What would they continue doing even if it were not on a university application?

## Summer programs: start paying attention

Many competitive summer programs have applications opening in Grade 10. These programs matter for several reasons. They provide substantive academic experience outside school. They signal genuine intellectual interest. For selective US universities, participation in well-regarded summer programs (particularly research programs, pre-college courses at universities, or specialist programs in specific fields) can add meaningful depth to an application.

Examples of programs that students in Grade 10 may be eligible for include:

- Research Science Institute (RSI) — Grade 11 students but Grade 10 students should note and plan
- MIT Primes and similar research programs
- Pre-college programs at universities (many open to Grade 10 students or rising Grade 11 students)
- Specialized programs in engineering, law, humanities, or medicine

Families should start researching which programs align with the student's interests, check eligibility, and understand application timelines. Some of the most competitive programs have January or February deadlines for summer entry, which means the planning needs to happen in the fall of Grade 10.

## Exploring US and UK university pathways

Grade 10 is a good time to start forming a general sense of whether a student's ambitions lean toward US universities, UK universities, or both. The two systems are substantially different.

US university applications are holistic. They consider academics, test scores, extracurriculars, essays, and letters of recommendation. Students apply to multiple schools across a range from reach to safety. Applications typically open in August of Grade 12 through the Common App or Coalition App, with early deadlines in November and regular deadlines in January.

UK university applications are course-specific and academic-led. Students apply through UCAS to up to five courses, which may be at different universities. Oxford and Cambridge have an October 15 deadline in Year 13. A-Level or IB predicted grades from teachers are central to the offers received. Personal statements are course-focused rather than character-focused.

A student with strong academic results and a clear intellectual passion may be well-suited to UK applications, particularly Oxbridge. A student with broad strengths, compelling extracurriculars, and a desire to keep options open may find the US system a better fit. Many students pursue both systems simultaneously.

Understanding this landscape in Grade 10 means the student and family can make informed decisions about curriculum choices, testing, and summer planning rather than discovering mid-Grade 11 that the course selections made do not align with the intended path.

## Common mistakes in Grade 10

**Waiting to think about any of this until Grade 11.** Grade 11 is when everything accelerates simultaneously: harder courses, standardized testing, extracurricular commitments, and the beginning of serious college research. Entering that year without having done any prior thinking makes it harder.

**Over-programming the student.** Grade 10 is not the time to schedule every weekend with enrichment activities. Students who are exhausted and overextended do not perform better academically or develop clearer direction. Focused, meaningful activity beats a packed schedule.

**Treating GCSEs (for UK-system students) as lower priority.** GCSE results feed into predicted grades. Students in Year 11 who underperform and hope to compensate later are making a harder path for themselves.

**Assuming the student needs to know exactly what they want to study.** They do not. But they should be developing a genuine sense of what interests them, which requires engaging with ideas, following curiosity, and having conversations rather than waiting for a career interest to arrive fully formed.

## FAQ

**Does Grade 10 actually matter for university applications?**  
Grade 10 itself does not appear heavily on most US university transcripts, but the decisions made in Grade 10 — course choices, extracurricular focus, testing awareness — directly shape what Grade 11 looks like. For UK students, Year 11 GCSE performance matters significantly for predicted grades.

**Should my child take the SAT or ACT in Grade 10?**  
Most students are not ready to sit the SAT or ACT in a high-stakes way during Grade 10. The PSAT in Grade 10 is a useful and low-pressure diagnostic. Formal SAT or ACT preparation typically begins in earnest in Grade 11.

**How many extracurricular activities should a Grade 10 student have?**  
Quality and genuine engagement matter more than quantity. A student with two or three meaningful commitments where they are growing in responsibility is better positioned than a student with eight superficial ones. Grade 10 is the right time to start thinking about which activities actually matter to the student.

**Is Grade 10 too early to start researching specific universities?**  
Not at all. Starting to form a general picture of whether the student leans toward US or UK universities, or what kinds of programs interest them, is useful background knowledge. It does not need to become an obsession, but early awareness shapes better decisions over time.

**What summer programs are available for Grade 10 students?**  
Many pre-college programs at universities accept students who are completing Grade 10 or rising into Grade 11. Research programs, debate institutes, writing seminars, and specialized academic programs vary by field. Families should research what is available in the student's areas of interest and note application deadlines, which often fall in winter.

**What is the biggest thing families get wrong about Grade 10 planning?**  
Treating it as either too early to bother or too late to relax. Grade 10 is a year to build good habits, make thoughtful choices, and develop genuine interests. The students who arrive at Grade 11 well-prepared are usually the ones who used Grade 10 to think clearly, not to do everything at once.
