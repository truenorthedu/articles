## Introduction

For many families in Asia, the question of boarding school comes up not as a whim but as a genuine educational decision. Perhaps your child is academically strong and needs more challenge. Perhaps you want them to experience an English-language environment before university. Or perhaps you are already thinking about Ivy League or Oxbridge admissions and want to understand how a boarding school education fits into that longer-term picture.

Whatever the reason, boarding school admissions is a process that rewards early planning. Both the US and UK systems have their own structures, timelines, and expectations. This guide explains how each works, what schools are looking for, and how international families can approach the process with clarity.

## US boarding schools: how the admissions process works

### Who applies and when

Most students apply to US boarding schools for entry at Grade 9 (around age 14) or Grade 10. Some schools also accept students at Grade 11, though spots at this stage are more limited. Families should begin researching schools in Grade 7 or Grade 8 to leave time for school visits, test preparation, and building a competitive application.

Application deadlines for most US boarding schools fall between January and February for September entry. This means the active application period runs through the fall of the year before entry. For a student hoping to start in September of Grade 9, the family should be preparing applications from September through January of Grade 8.

### The SSAT

The standard entrance assessment for US boarding schools is the SSAT (Secondary School Admission Test). It tests verbal reasoning, reading comprehension, and mathematics. Scores are reported on a percentile scale relative to other SSAT test-takers, not against a general population. This matters because the pool of students sitting the SSAT skews academically strong. A student scoring in the 75th percentile is performing well above average by most measures.

International students typically sit the SSAT in the fall of their application year. Some schools also accept the ISEE (Independent School Entrance Exam), and a small number accept standardized tests in the local language or system for early years of entry. Confirm the requirements with each school directly.

English language proficiency is assessed through SSAT verbal scores, writing samples, and sometimes TOEFL or other language tests. Schools want to know a student can engage fully in an English-language academic and residential environment.

### What US boarding schools evaluate

Beyond test scores, US boarding schools review academic transcripts (typically two to three years of prior school records), teacher recommendations (usually two to three), and a personal statement or short essay. Many schools also conduct applicant interviews, either in person or online. Interviews carry real weight. Schools are assessing fit: whether this student will contribute to the community, engage with peers and faculty, and make the most of the environment.

Character, curiosity, and contribution matter as much as grades. Schools at this level are building residential communities, not just academic classes. A student who is academically solid but brings genuine energy to music, debate, community service, or athletics may be a stronger candidate than a perfect-score student with no clear sense of self.

Financial aid is available at many US boarding schools, including some of the most selective. Families should investigate aid eligibility early in the process. Schools like Exeter, Andover, and Choate commit significant resources to financial aid programs, including for international students.

## UK boarding schools: a different structure

### Entry points and how they differ

UK boarding schools operate on a different entry structure. The main points of entry are:

- **11-plus (Year 7, age 11):** Common in the prep school system. Some secondary schools take students at this stage.
- **13-plus (Year 9, age 13):** The most common entry point for senior independent schools in England and Wales.
- **Sixth form (Year 12, age 16):** For the final two years of school (A-Levels or IB), some students enter from outside, including from overseas.

For competitive schools at 13-plus, families often need to register interest two to three years before entry. This is not a bureaucratic formality. Some schools have a registration deadline followed by an assessment phase, then a conditional offer that holds a place until the student sits the Common Entrance exam in Year 8.

### Common Entrance and school-specific assessments

The Common Entrance exam at 13-plus is a set of subject-specific papers covering English, mathematics, science, and optional subjects. It is sat by students in their final year of prep school (Year 8) and the results are sent directly to the senior school. Each senior school sets its own pass mark and grade thresholds. In practice, requirements vary depending on how selective the school is — families should confirm the expected standard directly with each school.

Some schools, particularly the most selective ones such as Eton and Winchester, run their own entrance assessments rather than relying solely on Common Entrance. These can include aptitude tests, additional interviews, and written exercises. Families should check each school's requirements carefully.

At 11-plus, students typically sit the school's own entrance exam and interview. The Common Entrance process does not apply at this stage.

International students who have not been through the British prep school system may need to navigate Common Entrance from outside. Some tutoring support is available, and a number of boarding schools have direct entry routes for international applicants who did not attend a UK prep school.

### English language requirements for UK schools

UK boarding schools expect strong English proficiency. Many will require IELTS or another language assessment if English is not the student's first language. Some schools have English language support programs but are selective about which students they admit into them. It is worth understanding what a school's EAL (English as an Additional Language) provision looks like before applying.

## What both systems have in common

Despite the structural differences, US and UK boarding schools share several characteristics in what they look for.

**Academic readiness.** Both systems want students who can handle the academic demands. For US schools, this is partly demonstrated through SSAT results and transcripts. For UK schools, it is demonstrated through Common Entrance performance, prior school reports, and in some cases aptitude tests.

**Character and contribution.** Both systems are building communities. Schools want students who will engage, take initiative, and contribute meaningfully, not just achieve on paper.

**Fit.** This is perhaps the most important factor that families underestimate. A school that is right for one student may be wrong for another. School visits, where possible, help families understand the culture, pace, and environment of each school. Interviews help schools assess whether a student is genuinely interested rather than just attracted to a name.

**Long-term academic planning.** Parents choosing boarding school are usually thinking beyond the school itself. They are thinking about what a boarding school education leads to. US boarding schools have strong track records placing students into selective US universities. UK independent schools have deep relationships with Oxbridge and Russell Group universities. Understanding this context helps families make better choices.

## Common mistakes families make

**Applying too late.** For UK schools at 13-plus, starting the process at Year 7 or even Year 8 is often too late. Registration deadlines at competitive schools can fall two to three years before entry.

**Treating it as a brand exercise.** Some families pursue the most recognizable school names without considering fit. A student who struggles in a highly pressured environment will not thrive regardless of the school's reputation.

**Underestimating the interview.** Both systems value the interview. Students who have not practiced articulating their interests, motivations, and character in English often underperform relative to their academic ability.

**Not exploring financial aid.** Many families assume international students are ineligible for aid. At many US schools, this is not the case. Financial aid is worth investigating seriously.

## FAQ

**What is the SSAT and how important is it for US boarding school admissions?**  
The SSAT (Secondary School Admission Test) is the standard entrance exam for most US boarding schools. It tests verbal reasoning, reading, and mathematics. It is one important factor in admissions but not the only one. Schools consider it alongside transcripts, recommendations, essays, and interviews.

**How far in advance should we start preparing for UK boarding school?**  
For entry at 13-plus, families should begin researching and registering with schools at least two to three years before the intended entry year. For entry at 11-plus, one to two years of preparation is typical. The most competitive schools fill places early.

**Do US boarding schools offer financial aid to international students?**  
Many do. Financial aid at US boarding schools is need-based. Some of the most selective schools, including Exeter and Andover, have significant endowments and commit meaningful aid to international applicants. Families should apply for aid at the same time as applying for admission.

**What is Common Entrance and who sits it?**  
Common Entrance is a set of subject-specific exams taken by students in Year 8 (age 13) in the UK system. It is typically sat by students leaving prep school and applying to senior independent schools. International students who did not attend a UK prep school may have alternative entry routes depending on the school.

**Do boarding schools care about extracurricular activities?**  
Yes. Both US and UK boarding schools value what a student contributes beyond academics. Sports, music, drama, art, debating, and community involvement all matter. Schools are looking for students who bring energy and character to the residential community.

**Can an international student apply to both US and UK boarding schools at the same time?**  
Yes, and many families do explore both systems simultaneously. The timelines, processes, and assessments differ considerably, so it helps to map them out clearly before starting. Working with an experienced consultant can help families manage both tracks without confusion.
